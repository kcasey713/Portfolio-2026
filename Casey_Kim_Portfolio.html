Agronomic Philosophy

Championship conditioning is built on discipline, not spectacle.

Every great course is the daily product of clear standards, sound cultural practice, and a crew that has been given the training and the authority to deliver them.

I have spent a quarter-century leading agronomic programs at elite private clubs and high-profile resort facilities across four climate zones. The thread across every role has been the same: elevate conditioning standards while building a team capable of sustaining them.

The work is technical — species selection, MLSN-aligned fertility, cultural practice scheduling, irrigation audit discipline, disease pressure management, tree and canopy strategy, drainage, bunker flash restoration, fleet planning. But it is first an operational discipline. Great agronomy is what happens when the right standards are set, the crew is trained and trusted to execute them, and capital and labor are deliberately allocated against the problems that actually matter.

At Ojai Valley Inn, that meant rebuilding the program from first principles beginning in 2022 — reinforcing daily setup standards, launching a section-based accountability framework, implementing a preventive fungicide program, refining MARS (Minimal Aeration for Resilient Surfaces) on greens, modernizing the irrigation injection system, and naming the long-term capital priorities with a straight face. That same discipline shaped the grow-ins at Somerby and Indian Wells, the transition work at Montecito, and the tournament deliveries I led at Indian Wells Golf Resort during its Skins Game and Golf Channel years.

Featured Work — 2022 to Present

Rebuilding the agronomic program at a 5-star California resort.

Four years of program development at Ojai Valley Inn — foundation, refinement, and strategic shift — documented across three annual reviews.

Director of Agronomy

Ojai Valley Inn

2022 — Present  ·  Ojai, CA

Five-star historic resort. 18-hole course designed by George Thomas in 1923. Recipient of numerous hospitality excellence awards. Direct responsibility for all agronomic operations, capital planning, team development, and stewardship alignment with the resort's broader brand.

2023 — Foundation year. Arrived and rebuilt the operating model around cross-training, section-based accountability, and daily setup standards. Launched the tree removal program. Installed on-site weather stations. Upgraded to Cirrus Pro irrigation software. Revitalized the injection system. Delivered the California Women's Championship to full tournament conditioning in the wettest year on record — 42.17 inches of precipitation, including back-to-back atmospheric rivers, five consecutive closure days, and zero permanent turf loss. Initiated the Audubon Cooperative Sanctuary registration.

2024 — Program refinement. Implemented the MARS (Minimal Aeration for Resilient Surfaces) program on greens. Formally documented bunker maintenance as the department's single largest labor demand. Wrote the business case for a comprehensive bunker redesign. Continued the tree removal program and moved the Audubon program from registration to active compliance. Refined the proxy (paclobutrazol) program for annual bluegrass suppression. Course conditions held through variable weather and all events were hosted without turf setbacks.

2025 — Strategic shift. Delivered the #11 green recovery program — the most intensive single-green intervention of the four-year arc, with documented improvement from targeted overseeding, sand topdressing, and cultural practice adjustment. Refined greens to 0.110–0.120 inches. Promoted Jesus Samayoa to Equipment Operator — the clearest internal development outcome of the program. Acquired the large-area aerifier. Completed the autonomous mower feasibility assessment. Framed the three-decision ownership conversation: bunker reconstruction, Hole 13 subsurface drainage, and autonomous mower integration.

Leadership & Mentorship

Teams that deliver — and the people who go on to lead them.

The clearest evidence of a program is the trajectory of the people inside it. At OVI, that looks like Luis Rocelio Becerra's promotion to Equipment Operator in 2023 and Jesus Samayoa's promotion to Equipment Operator in 2025 — two direct outcomes of the cross-training and standards culture put in place on arrival.

Across the broader career, four former direct reports now lead their own programs as Golf Course Superintendents at desert and upper-Midwest facilities. Several more are established in operator and assistant-superintendent roles.

I believe in a team that is cross-trained across greens, tee/approach, fairway, and rough operations; that is held to daily setup standards and section-based accountability; that is given clear training, equipment, and authority; and that is supported with safety discipline — pre-shift calisthenics, Active Assailant Preparedness Training, protective footwear programs, and heat-illness prevention among them.

The work a maintenance crew does is the most visible product of the agronomy program. If I've done my job, the course shows it and the team can explain why.
